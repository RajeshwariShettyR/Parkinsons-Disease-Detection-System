Symptoms of Parkinson's Disease

1. Motor Symptoms

The most recognizable symptoms of Parkinson's Disease are motor-related and include:

  • Tremors: Involuntary shaking, often starting in the hands or fingers. It is more noticeable when at rest.
  • Bradykinesia: Slowness of movement, making simple tasks take longer than usual.
  • Rigidity: Stiffness in the muscles that can limit the range of motion and cause pain.
  • Postural Instability: Poor balance and coordination, which can increase the risk of falls.
  • Freezing Episodes: A temporary inability to move, particularly when initiating walking.

2. Non-Motor Symptoms

In addition to motor symptoms, Parkinson's Disease can lead to a variety of non-motor symptoms, including:

  • Sleep Disorders: Difficulty sleeping, vivid dreams, or daytime sleepiness.
  • Depression and Anxiety: Emotional changes and mood swings.
  • Loss of Smell: Early loss of sense of smell (anosmia).
  • Constipation: Slower digestive processes.
  • Cognitive Impairment: Issues with memory, thinking, and decision-making, especially in later stages.
  • Fatigue: Persistent tiredness unrelated to activity levels.

How is Parkinson's Disease Diagnosed?

Diagnosing Parkinson's Disease is a clinical process since no single test can confirm its presence. Doctors rely on a combination of medical history, symptoms, and tests:

  • Neurological Examination: A physical assessment of movement, reflexes, and coordination.
  • Medical History: Identifying symptoms and any family history of neurological disorders.
  • Imaging Tests: Although not used to diagnose Parkinson's, MRI or CT scans may rule out other conditions.
  • Dopamine Transporter Scan (DaTscan): A specialized imaging test to assess dopamine-producing neurons.
  • Response to Medication: Improvement of symptoms after starting Parkinson's medications can confirm the diagnosis.
